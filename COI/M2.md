## M2 P1 to P3

### Key terms and meaning

- Sovereign
  - India is independent and no other country controls its decisions.

- Socialist
  - The government tries to reduce inequality and provide equal opportunities and welfare for all people.

- Secular
  - All religions are treated equally, and people are free to follow any religion or no religion.

- Democratic
  - People choose their government by voting in elections.

- Republic
  - The head of the country (President) is elected, not a king or queen by birth.

- Justice
  - Everyone should get fair treatment socially, economically, and politically.

- Liberty
  - People have freedom of thought, speech, belief, and expression.

- Equality
  - All people are equal before the law and should get equal opportunities.

- Fraternity
  - It means brotherhood and unity among all people of the country.

### LENGTHIEST WRITTEN CONSTITUTION (Simple Explanation)

- Original Constitution (1949)
  - Preamble
  - 395 Articles
  - 22 Parts
  - 8 Schedules

- Present Constitution
  - Preamble
  - Around 470 Articles
  - 25 Parts
  - 12 Schedules

### REASONS WHY THE INDIAN CONSTITUTION IS VERY LONG

1. Geographical and Diversity Factors
   - India is very large and has many languages, religions, and cultures.
   - Detailed rules were needed to manage such diversity.

2. Historical Factors
   - Many provisions were taken from the Government of India Act, 1935.
   - That Act itself was very detailed and lengthy.

3. Single Constitution for Centre and States
   - One Constitution is used for both Union and State governments.

4. Presence of Legal Experts
   - Many legal scholars and lawyers were part of the Constituent Assembly.
   - They included detailed legal and administrative provisions.

5. Detailed Administrative Provisions
   - Constitution explains administration, powers, elections, emergencies, etc. in detail.

## M2 P4 to P6

### UNITARY FEATURES OF THE INDIAN UNION (Simple Explanation)

India is called a federal country, but the Centre has more power in many situations.
These are called unitary features.

- Constitution's Flexibility
  - Some parts of the Constitution can be changed easily by Parliament.

- Power with the Centre
  - Central Government has stronger powers compared to States.

- Unequal Rajya Sabha Representation
  - States do not get equal seats in Rajya Sabha.
  - Bigger states get more representation.

- Executive–Legislature Link
  - Ministers are part of the legislature as well as executive.

- Lok Sabha vs Rajya Sabha
  - Lok Sabha is more powerful in many matters.

- Emergency Powers
  - During emergency, Centre can take control over State powers.

- Integrated Judiciary
  - Single judicial system for whole country.
  - Supreme Court is at the top.

- Single Citizenship
  - Only one citizenship: Indian citizenship.
  - Unlike USA, there is no separate state citizenship.

- Governor’s Appointment
  - Governors of states are appointed by the President (Centre).

- State Formation
  - Parliament can create or change states.

- All India Services
  - IAS, IPS officers work for both Centre and States.

- Integrated Election Machinery
  - Election Commission conducts elections for entire country.

- Veto on State Bills
  - Governor/President can stop certain state laws.

- Integrated Audit Machinery
  - CAG audits accounts of both Centre and States.

### PARLIAMENTARY FORM OF GOVERNMENT (Simple Explanation)

- India follows the British Parliamentary System
  - Not the American Presidential System.

- Exists at:
  - Centre
  - States

#### MAIN FEATURES

1. Nominal and Real Executives
   - President/Governor = nominal head
   - Prime Minister/Chief Minister = real executive power

2. Majority Party Rule
   - Party with majority forms government.

3. Collective Responsibility
   - Council of Ministers is jointly responsible to Lok Sabha.

4. Ministers from Legislature
   - Ministers are usually members of Parliament/Legislature.

5. Leadership of PM/CM
   - Prime Minister or Chief Minister leads the government.

6. Dissolution of Lower House
   - Lok Sabha or State Assembly can be dissolved before term ends.

### SYNTHESIS OF PARLIAMENTARY SOVEREIGNTY AND JUDICIAL SUPREMACY
(Simple Explanation)

- Britain follows Parliamentary Sovereignty
  - Parliament is supreme.

- America follows Judicial Supremacy
  - Supreme Court has very strong powers.

- India follows a balance (synthesis) of both systems.

#### IMPORTANT POINT

- Indian Constitution uses:
  - “Procedure Established by Law” (Article 21)
  - Instead of American “Due Process of Law”

### BALANCE OF POWERS IN INDIA

1. Power of Supreme Court
   - Supreme Court can declare laws unconstitutional.
   - This is called Judicial Review.

2. Power of Parliament
   - Parliament can amend most parts of the Constitution.

**FINAL UNDERSTANDING:**
India maintains balance:
- Parliament is powerful
- But courts can check unconstitutional laws
- Neither has absolute power

## M2 P7 to P10

### PROCEDURE ESTABLISHED BY LAW vs DUE PROCESS OF LAW
(Simple Understanding)

#### BASIC IDEA

- Procedure Established by Law
  - Government action is valid if it follows the legal procedure written in law.

- Due Process of Law
  - Not only procedure, but the law itself must also be fair, just, and reasonable.

#### DIFFERENCE IN SIMPLE WORDS

1. Origin

- Procedure Established by Law
  - From British legal system

- Due Process of Law
  - From American legal system

2. Indian Constitution Reference

- Article 21:
  - “No person shall be deprived of life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law.”

- Due Process not directly written in Indian Constitution
  - But later included through Supreme Court interpretation.

3. Main Focus

- Procedure Established by Law
  - Focuses on following correct legal steps.

- Due Process of Law
  - Focuses on fairness of both:
    - Procedure
    - Law itself

4. Judicial Review

- Procedure Established by Law
  - Courts check:
    - Was the procedure followed correctly?

- Due Process of Law
  - Courts check:
    - Was procedure followed?
    - Is the law fair and reasonable?

5. Protection Level

- Procedure Established by Law
  - Narrower protection

- Due Process of Law
  - Broader protection for citizens

6. Effect on Fundamental Rights

- Procedure Established by Law
  - Even unfair laws may survive if procedure is followed.

- Due Process of Law
  - Arbitrary or unfair laws can be struck down.

#### IMPORTANT INDIAN CASES

1. A.K. Gopalan Case (1950)
   - Article 21 interpreted narrowly.
   - Only procedure mattered.

2. Maneka Gandhi Case (1978)
   - Huge change.
   - Supreme Court said:
     - Procedure must be:
       - Right
       - Just
       - Fair
     - Not arbitrary or oppressive.

   - India moved closer to “Due Process of Law”.

**SHORT MEMORY TRICK**

- Britain → Procedure
- America → Fairness + Procedure
- India → Started with Procedure, later expanded toward Due Process

### INTEGRATED AND INDEPENDENT JUDICIARY
(Simple Explanation)

- India has one integrated judicial system.

#### STRUCTURE OF COURTS

1. Supreme Court
   - Highest court in India

2. High Courts
   - State-level courts

3. Subordinate Courts
   - District and lower courts under High Courts

#### IMPORTANT FEATURES

- Same court system handles:
  - Central laws
  - State laws

- Supreme Court functions:
  - Federal Court
  - Highest Court of Appeal
  - Protector of Fundamental Rights
  - Guardian of Constitution

- Independence of Judiciary
  - Judges are protected from political pressure.

#### PROVISIONS ENSURING INDEPENDENCE

- Security of tenure
  - Judges cannot be removed easily.

- Fixed service conditions
  - Salary and conditions protected.

### FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
(Simple + Easy to Remember)

Part III of Indian Constitution guarantees 6 Fundamental Rights:

1. Right to Equality (Articles 14–18)
   - Equality before law, no discrimination

2. Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22)
   - Freedom of speech, movement, etc.

3. Right against Exploitation (Articles 23–24)
   - Prevents human trafficking and child labour

4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28)
   - Freedom to follow and practice religion

5. Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30)
   - Protects language and culture of minorities

6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)
   - Right to approach courts for protection of rights
   - Called the “Heart and Soul” of Constitution by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

**SUPER SHORT MEMORY LINE:**
Equality → Freedom → Exploitation → Religion → Culture/Education → Remedies

## M2 P11 to end

### DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES OF STATE POLICY (DPSP)
(Simple Explanation)

- Found in:
  - Part IV of the Indian Constitution

- Meaning:
  - These are guidelines given to the government for running the country in a welfare-oriented way.

- Goal:
  - Help create a fair, just, and welfare-based society.

### CLASSIFICATION OF DPSP

1. Socialistic Principles
   - Focus on social and economic equality
   - Example:
     - Reducing inequality
     - Welfare of workers

2. Gandhian Principles
   - Based on Mahatma Gandhi’s ideas
   - Example:
     - Village panchayats
     - Cottage industries

3. Liberal-Intellectual Principles
   - Focus on freedom, justice, and modern ideas
   - Example:
     - Uniform Civil Code
     - International peace

### IMPORTANT FEATURES

- Non-justiciable
  - Citizens cannot directly go to court to enforce them.

- Fundamental in governance
  - Even though courts cannot enforce them, governments should follow them while making policies.

### DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES (EXAMPLES)

1. Free and compulsory education
   - Education for children should be provided.

2. Promotion of SC/ST welfare
   - Improve conditions of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes.

3. Organization of village panchayats
   - Encourage local self-government in villages.

4. Separation of judiciary from executive
   - Courts should work independently from government control.

5. Uniform Civil Code
   - Same civil laws for all citizens regardless of religion.

6. Environmental protection
   - Protect forests, wildlife, and environment.

7. International peace
   - Promote friendly relations among countries.

### FUNDAMENTAL DUTIES
(Simple Explanation)

- Added by:
  - 42nd Amendment Act, 1976

- Suggested by:
  - Swaran Singh Committee

- Additional duty added later by:
  - 86th Amendment

- Located in:
  - Part IV-A
  - Article 51A

- Nature:
  - Non-justiciable
  - Courts cannot force citizens to perform them.

### MEANING OF FUNDAMENTAL DUTIES

- These are moral responsibilities of citizens towards the country.

Examples:
- Respect Constitution and National Symbols
- Protect environment
- Promote harmony
- Safeguard public property

**SHORT MEMORY TRICK**

- Fundamental Rights → Rights of citizens
- DPSP → Duties of government
- Fundamental Duties → Duties of citizens

**FUNDAMENTAL DUTIES**
- These are moral responsibilities of every Indian citizen towards the nation.

### LIST OF FUNDAMENTAL DUTIES (Easy Understanding)

1. Respect Constitution, National Flag, and National Anthem
   - Follow constitutional ideals and respect national symbols.

2. Follow ideals of freedom struggle
   - Remember and practice values that inspired independence movement.

3. Protect sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India
   - Protect the unity and strength of the country.

4. Defend the country when required
   - Serve the nation during national need.

5. Promote harmony and brotherhood
   - Maintain unity beyond religion, language, region, etc.
   - Respect dignity of women.

6. Preserve cultural heritage
   - Protect India’s rich culture and traditions.

7. Protect environment
   - Care for forests, rivers, lakes, wildlife, and living creatures.

8. Develop scientific temper and humanism
   - Think logically, scientifically, and support reforms.

9. Safeguard public property and avoid violence
   - Protect public resources and maintain peace.

10. Strive for excellence
   - Work towards improvement in personal and national life.

11. Provide education to children (6–14 years)
   - Parents/guardians should ensure children receive education.
   - Added by 86th Amendment (2002).

**SHORT MEMORY FLOW:**
Respect → Freedom ideals → Protect India → Defend Nation → Harmony → Culture → Environment → Scientific Thinking → Public Property → Excellence → Education

### A SECULAR STATE
(Simple Explanation)

- India has no official religion.
  - Government does not support one religion over another.

- Equal respect to all religions
  - Every religion is treated equally.

- Political equality
  - People of all religions have equal political rights.

- Positive secularism
  - Government respects all religions and may support welfare activities of all communities equally.

### UNIVERSAL ADULT FRANCHISE
(Simple Explanation)

- Every citizen aged 18 or above can vote.

- No discrimination based on:
  - Caste
  - Religion
  - Race
  - Sex
  - Wealth
  - Literacy

- Voting age change:
  - Earlier: 21 years
  - Reduced to 18 years by:
    - 61st Constitutional Amendment Act, 1988
    - Effective from 1989

### SINGLE CITIZENSHIP
(Simple Explanation)

- India provides only one citizenship:
  - Indian Citizenship

- Unlike USA:
  - USA has dual citizenship:
    - Country citizenship
    - State citizenship

- In India:
  - A person is only a citizen of India, not of a particular state.

EXAMPLE:
- A person living in Karnataka is not a separate citizen of Karnataka.
- He/she is simply an Indian citizen.

#### ADVANTAGES

- Can work anywhere in India
- Equal rights across the country
- Reduces regionalism
- Promotes national unity

### INDEPENDENT BODIES
(Simple Explanation)

The Constitution created independent institutions to protect democracy.

1. Election Commission
   - Conducts free and fair elections.

2. Comptroller and Auditor-General (CAG)
   - Audits government accounts.

3. Union Public Service Commission (UPSC)
   - Conducts exams for All India and Central Services.

4. State Public Service Commission (SPSC)
   - Conducts recruitment exams for state services.

### EMERGENCY PROVISIONS
(Simple Explanation)

1. National Emergency – Article 352
   - During war, external attack, or armed rebellion.

2. State Emergency – Article 356
   - Failure of constitutional machinery in a state.
   - President’s Rule imposed.

3. Financial Emergency – Article 360
   - Financial instability in the country.

### THREE-TIER GOVERNMENT
(Simple Explanation)

- 73rd Amendment
  - Panchayats (Rural local government)

- 74th Amendment
  - Municipalities (Urban local government)

#### LEVELS OF GOVERNMENT:
1. Central Government
2. State Government
3. Local Government

### CO-OPERATIVE SOCIETIES
(Simple Explanation)

- 97th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2011
  - Gave constitutional status to co-operative societies.

#### IMPORTANT PROVISIONS

1. Article 19(1)(c)
   - Right to form cooperative societies became a Fundamental Right.

2. Article 43B
   - Government should promote cooperative societies.

3. Part IX-B
   - Rules for formation and management of cooperative societies.

### CONCLUSION (Simple Understanding)

- Indian Constitution is:
  - Dynamic and adaptable
  - Combination of rigidity and flexibility
  - Federal but with unitary features
  - Ensures democratic governance and protection of rights

**SUPER SHORT FINAL REVISION:**
Rights + Duties + Democracy + Strong Centre + Independent Judiciary + Welfare + Unity
